\section{Reproducing results}
The normal state Hamiltonian describing the copper peroxide plane with zinc impurity doping is given by
\begin{multline}
    \hat{\mathcal{H}}_{ 0 } \equiv
        \sum_{-N/2\leq x,y\leq N/2}\sum_{\sigma\in\{\uparrow,\downarrow\}}\sum_{m\in\{d, p_x, p_y\}}\left[
            -\mu_m \hat{c}^{ \sigma,m \dagger}_{ x,y }\hat{c}^{ \sigma,m }_{ x,y }
        \right]
            + \sum_{-L/2\leq x,y\leq L/2}\sum_{\sigma\in\{\uparrow,\downarrow\}} \left[+V\hat{c}^{ \sigma,d \dagger}_{ x,y }\hat{c}^{ \sigma,d }_{ x,y }\right]
            \\
        +\sum_{-N/2\leq x,y\leq N/2}\sum_{\sigma\in\{\uparrow,\downarrow\}}\left[
            +t \hat{c}^{ \sigma,d \dagger}_{ x,y }\hat{c}^{ \sigma,p_x }_{ x,y }
            -t \hat{c}^{ \sigma,d \dagger}_{ x,y }\hat{c}^{ \sigma,p_y }_{ x,y }
            -t \hat{c}^{ \sigma, p_x \dagger}_{ x,y }\hat{c}^{ \sigma,d }_{ x+1,y }
            +t \hat{c}^{ \sigma,p_y \dagger}_{ x,y }\hat{c}^{ \sigma,d }_{ x,y+1 }
        \right]
\end{multline}
where the fermion operator $\hat{c}^{\sigma,m\dagger}_{ x,y }$ creates an operator on atomic orbital $m\in\{d_{x^2-y^2},p_x,p_y\}$ within the unit cell located at $\mathbf{R}_{x,y}= x \mathbf{b}_0\hat{x}+y \mathbf{b}_1 \hat{y}$,
where $\mathbf{b}_0, \mathbf{b}_1$ are the lattice basis vectors,
$\hat{n}^{(\alpha)\dagger}_{ x,y }$ is the number operator, $\mu_d\equiv\mu$ and $\mu_{p_x}\equiv\mu_{p_y}\equiv\mu+s$ are the chemical potentials of the copper and oxygen orbitals and $s$ is a rigid shift of the atomic potentials.
The parameter $V$ sets the coupling strength of a zinc impurity at the centre of our lattice, and whose size goes from a single site $L=0$ to a cluster $L>0$.
In the minimal theory, we assume half-filling $\mu=s=0$ and zero coupling $V=0$.
The interorbital, intra- and inter-unit-cell hoppings strengths are given by $t=1$, which normalises the model paramters.
The boundary conditions are taken to be periodic.
The lattice is depicted with $N=3$ unit cells in Figure \ref{fig:2D-SSH_unit_cell}. 

The interacting part of the minimal theory describes a singlet electron-electron interaction on the copper sites, but not on the zinc-doped sites
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\mathcal{V}}\equiv
    \sum_{-N/2\leq x,y\leq N/2}\sum_{m\in\{d,p_x,p_y\}}\left[
    +U^m_{x,y}  \hat{c}^{ \uparrow,m \dagger}_{ x,y }\hat{c}^{ \downarrow,m \dagger}_{ x,y } \hat{c}^{ \uparrow,m }_{ x,y }\hat{c}^{ \downarrow,m }_{ x,y }\right],
\end{equation}
where $U^m_{x,y}$ is the strength of the Hubbard interaction on orbital $m$, which, in the 
simplest theory, we let $U^m_{x,y}:= U$ for $L/2 < x,y \leq N/2$ and $U^m_{x,y}:= 0$ for $0\leq x,y \leq L/2$.
. $U>0$ describes Coulomb repulsion and $U<0$ is the attractive BCS (singlet) scenario.
The minimal model describing Zn-doped CuO$_2$ superconducting planes with BCS-like interactions on the Cu sites is then
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\mathcal{H}}\equiv
    \hat{\mathcal{H}}_0
    +\hat{\mathcal{V}}
\end{equation}
We solve this Hamiltonian with inhomogeneous lattice self-consistent mean-field theory.

\section{Self-consistent mean-field solution}
The Bogoliubov-de Gennes (BdG) mean-field Hamiltonian is
\begin{multline}
    \hat{\mathcal{V}}_\text{mf}=
        \sum_{\sigma\in\{\uparrow,\downarrow\}}\sum_{m\in\{d,p_x,p_y\}}\sum_{-N/2\leq x,y\leq N/2}\left[
    \phi_{x,y}^{\sigma,m}\hat{n}^{\sigma,m}_{ x,y }\right]
    \\
    +\sum_{m\in\{d,p_x,p_y\}}\sum_{-N/2\leq x,y\leq N/2}
    \left[
    \Phi_{x,y}^{m} \hat{c}^{ \uparrow,m }_{ x,y }\hat{c}^{ \downarrow,m }_{ x,y }
    +\Delta_{x,y}^{m} \hat{c}^{ \uparrow, m \dagger}_{ x,y }\hat{c}^{ \downarrow, m \dagger }_{ x,y }
    +\Delta_{x,y}^{m*} \hat{c}^{ \uparrow,m }_{ x,y }\hat{c}^{ \downarrow,m }_{ x,y }
    \right],
\end{multline}
where $\phi$ is the {\it Hartree} term which renormalises the chemical potential, allowing for a Peirl's instability. 
$\Phi$ is a {\it Fock} term, describing spin-flips on the $d$-orbitals.
The additional {\it Gorkov} terms $\Delta_v$ capture fermion-fermion pairing, relevant to theories of magnetism ($U>0$) and superconductivity ($U<0$).
These terms, solved self-consistently, are known as inhomogenous {\it (superconducting) density functional theory, SC-DFT}. 
The self-consistent solution to the mean field is the saddle point 
$\langle\hat{\mathcal{V}}\rangle_\text{mf} = \langle\hat{\mathcal{V}}_\text{mf}\rangle_\text{mf}$, in which the mean-field eigenstates $\{E_n,|n\rangle\}$ approximate the state of the interacting theory by minimising (or, unstably, maximising) the free energy, where $E_n\geq0$ are half the solutions to the Bogoliubov-de Gennes (BdG) Hamiltonian, and where the rest of the BdG spectrum $E_n\leq0$ are related by particle-hole symmetry.
The solutions are
\begin{eqnarray}
    \phi^{\uparrow,m}_{x,y} &=& +U^m_{x,y}\langle\hat{n}^{ \downarrow,m}_{ x,y }\rangle,\nonumber\\
    \phi^{\downarrow,m}_{x,y} &=& +U^m_{x,y}\langle\hat{n}^{ \uparrow,m}_{ x,y }\rangle,\nonumber\\
    \Phi^{m}_{x,y} &=& -U^m_{x,y}\langle\hat{c}^{ \uparrow,m \dagger }_{ x,y }\hat{c}^{ \downarrow,m }_{ x,y }\rangle,\nonumber\\
    \Delta^{m}_{x,y} &=& +U^m_{x,y}\langle\hat{c}^{ \uparrow,m }_{ x,y }\hat{c}^{ \downarrow,m }_{ x,y }\rangle,\nonumber\\
    \Delta^{m*}_{x,y} &=& +U^m_{x,y}\langle\hat{c}^{ \downarrow,m \dagger }_{ x,y }\hat{c}^{ \uparrow,m \dagger}_{ x,y }\rangle.
\end{eqnarray}
Note the sign of the Fock terms due Fermionic anticommutation.
From these equations, its clear that we expect the mean-fields to go to zero on the Zn sites.

\section{Free energy}
At zero temperature, the free energy of the interacting theory is 
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{F}=
    \mathcal{F}_\text{mf}
+\langle\mathcal{V}\rangle
-\langle\mathcal{V}_\text{mf}\rangle
    ,
\end{equation}
where the mean-field free energy is
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{F}_\text{mf}=E_g - 2 k_B T \sum_n\left[ \log{1+e^{-E_n/k_B T}}\right]
,
\end{equation}
where $T$ is the temperature of the thermal bath enclosing the system, $k_B$ is the Boltzmann constant, $E_n$ are the eigenvalues of the mean-field Bogoloiubov-de Gennes Hamiltonian $\mathcal{H}_\text{mf}$ and the ground state energy is given by
\begin{equation}
    E_g=\sum_n \left[E_n\right]  
    + \frac{1}{\mathcal{N}}\sum_\sigma\sum_m\sum_{x,y}\left[\langle \hat{n}_{x,y}^{\sigma,m} \rangle\right],
\end{equation}
and $\mathcal{N}$ is the number of degrees of freedom.
The quartic interaction
$\langle\mathcal{V}\rangle$
in the mean-field basis $\{E_n,|n\rangle\}$ is given by
\begin{multline}
    \langle\mathcal{V}\rangle=
    \frac{1}{\mathcal{N}}
    \sum_\sigma\sum_m\sum_{x,y}
    U^m_{x,y}
    \Big[
    \langle\hat{n}^{ \downarrow,m}_{ x,y }\rangle
    \langle\hat{n}^{ \uparrow,m}_{ x,y }\rangle
    -\langle\hat{c}^{ \uparrow,m \dagger }_{ x,y }\hat{c}^{ \downarrow,m }_{ x,y }\rangle
    \langle\hat{c}^{ \uparrow,m \dagger }_{ x,y }\hat{c}^{ \downarrow,m }_{ x,y }\rangle\\
    +\langle\hat{c}^{ \uparrow,m }_{ x,y }\hat{c}^{ \downarrow,m }_{ x,y }\rangle
    \langle\hat{c}^{ \downarrow,m \dagger }_{ x,y }\hat{c}^{ \uparrow,m \dagger}_{ x,y }\rangle\Big]
,
\end{multline}

The quadratic interaction
$\langle\mathcal{V}_\text{mf}\rangle$
is given by
\begin{multline}
    \langle\mathcal{V}_\text{mf}\rangle=
    \frac{1}{\mathcal{N}}
    \sum_\sigma\sum_m\sum_{x,y}
    \Big[
    \phi^{\uparrow,m}_{x,y} \langle\hat{n}^{ \downarrow,m}_{ x,y }\rangle
    +\phi^{\downarrow,m}_{x,y} \langle\hat{n}^{ \uparrow,m}_{ x,y }\rangle
    -\Phi^{m}_{x,y} \langle\hat{c}^{ \uparrow,m \dagger }_{ x,y }\hat{c}^{ \downarrow,m }_{ x,y }\rangle\\
    -\Phi^{m*}_{x,y} \langle\hat{c}^{ \uparrow,m \dagger }_{ x,y }\hat{c}^{ \downarrow,m }_{ x,y }\rangle
    +\Delta^{m}_{x,y} \langle\hat{c}^{ \uparrow,m }_{ x,y }\hat{c}^{ \downarrow,m }_{ x,y }\rangle
    +\Delta^{m*}_{x,y} \langle\hat{c}^{ \downarrow,m \dagger }_{ x,y }\hat{c}^{ \uparrow,m \dagger}_{ x,y }\rangle\Big]
,
\end{multline}


More details of the inhomogeous self-consistent superconducting density functional theory can be found in Leggett [{\color{red}REF}].

\section{Green's function}
\createfigure{2D-Weyl-SSH/unit_cell}{2D-SSH_unit_cell}

The solution to the Hamiltonian is given by the Green's function, which we remind the reader, is given by
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\mathcal{G}}(\omega-i\epsilon,x,y)
    \equiv
    \sum_n\left[
        \frac{\psi_n(x,y)\psi_n(x,y)^\dagger}{\omega-i\epsilon-E_n}
        \right],
\end{equation}
where $\psi_n(x,y)$ is the eigenvector associated with the $n^\text{th}$ eigenenergy, resolved at the point on the lattice $(x,y)$. 
The Green's function matrix has a component for each atom.
The Green's function is experimental observables through the {\it local density of states},
\begin{equation}
    \text{LDOS}(\omega;x,y)
    \equiv
    -\frac{1}{\pi}\Im\hat{\mathcal{G}}(\omega-i\epsilon,x,y)
,
\end{equation}
which is a measure of the density of states at a given point, at a specified applied bias energy $\omega$ and resolution $\epsilon$.
The local density of states for each atomic site with the unit cells are given in Figure \ref{fig:2D-SSH_ldos_each_atom}, showing the existence of zero energy edge states.

\createfigure{2D-Weyl-SSH/ldos_each_atom}{2D-SSH_ldos_each_atom}

Resolving the local density states at the level of the unit cell (summing over the atoms) and integrating over the $y$-axis, we study the {\it spectrum} of the system. 
The spectrum shows edge modes present at zero energy at the edges, which are not present in the bulk. These are distinct signatures of Majorana modes. 
In addition to localised density at the edge, there exists a `tail' of density which the authors investigate further.
